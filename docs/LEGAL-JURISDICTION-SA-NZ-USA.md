# Jurisdiction: South Africa, New Zealand, USA — summary and recommendation

**Context:** Most customers are in the USA. You asked whether South African law is a bad idea and how New Zealand compares. This doc summarizes applicable-law considerations and recommends a choice.

---

## South Africa

### Laws that can apply
- **POPIA** (Protection of Personal Information Act): applies when you process personal information of SA data subjects or are established in SA. Requires data subject rights, breach notification, cross-border transfer safeguards, operator/processor obligations, security measures. Your Privacy Policy should address POPIA if you have SA users.
- **ECTA** (Electronic Communications and Transactions Act) and **CPA** (Consumer Protection Act): apply to e‑commerce with SA consumers. They require clear disclosure (business details, price, terms, refund policy, privacy), and ECTA gives a **7-day cooling-off** for online transactions (with exceptions, e.g. digital content/software once performance has started).
- **Governing law:** SA companies often choose South African law in their terms. SA courts and lawyers are familiar with it; SA consumers get local protections.

### Why SA as governing law is a bad fit when most customers are USA
- **Enforcement:** US customers and US courts are not set up for SA law. Enforcing an SA judgment in the USA (or the other way around) is more complex and costly.
- **Familiarity:** US lawyers and users expect contracts with US customers to be governed by a US state’s law (e.g. Delaware, New York). SA law is unfamiliar and can look odd to a US-heavy user base.
- **Mandatory law:** You still have to comply with mandatory consumer and privacy law in each country where you have users (e.g. CCPA in California, POPIA for SA users). Choosing SA *governing law* does not remove those obligations for US users; it just makes the contract itself subject to SA law, which adds complexity without clear benefit if your base is US.

**Conclusion:** Using **South African law as the governing law** in your Terms is **not recommended** when most customers are in the USA. You should still comply with POPIA (and ECTA/CPA) for any South African customers.

## New Zealand

### Why NZ is sometimes chosen
- **Common law:** Familiar to English-speaking jurisdictions; predictable contract principles.
- **Neutral:** Used as a “neutral” governing law when parties are in different regions.
- **Freedom of choice:** NZ generally allows parties to choose a foreign governing law; some NZ mandatory law (e.g. Consumer Guarantees Act) may still apply for NZ consumers in certain cases.
- **Enforcement:** NZ judgments can be enforced in many jurisdictions under reciprocal arrangements, but US courts and lawyers are still more used to US state law.

### When NZ makes sense
- Company is **based in New Zealand**, or
- You want a **neutral common-law** seat and have a mix of US, EU, and other customers.

### When it’s less ideal
- **Most customers are in the USA:** US law (e.g. Delaware or New York) is then simpler, more predictable, and easier to enforce in the US. No strong reason to choose NZ over a US state in that case.

**Conclusion:** New Zealand is a **valid and reasonable** choice if you are NZ-based or want a neutral common-law jurisdiction. For a **USA-heavy customer base**, a **US state** as governing law is usually the better fit.

## USA (e.g. Delaware or New York)

### Why US governing law fits when most customers are USA
- **Standard practice:** B2B and B2C SaaS serving US customers commonly choose the law of a US state—often **Delaware** or the state where the company is based (e.g. **New York**).
- **Predictability:** Delaware has a well-developed body of contract and corporate law; New York is also widely used for commercial contracts. US lawyers and courts are familiar with both.
- **Enforcement:** Disputes with US customers can be resolved in US courts under US law; enforcement is straightforward domestically.
- **Investor/counterparty expectation:** If you ever raise capital or do B2B deals in the US, Delaware (or another US state) is what parties expect.

### What you still need to do
- **Privacy:** Comply with applicable law where users are (e.g. CCPA for California, GDPR for EU/EEA/UK, POPIA for SA). Your Privacy Policy already references these.
- **Consumer rights:** Some US states (and EU/UK/SA etc.) have mandatory consumer protections that cannot be overridden by a choice-of-law clause; your Terms already preserve that (e.g. “If you are in the EU/EEA or UK, you may also have statutory rights that are not limited by these terms”).

**Conclusion:** For manu2print, with **most customers in the USA**, **US governing law (e.g. the State of Delaware, USA)** is the **recommended** choice in §13 of the Terms. Use the state where you are incorporated or primarily operate if you prefer (e.g. New York, California).
